The State of Romance Demand

Three years of measured reader demand — not bestseller lists, not vibes. What romance readers asked for from July 2023 to June 2026, which tropes held their ground, which faded, and what is rising in every major lane right now.

Mid-2026 edition · Published 17 July 2026 · Based on 224,080 dated reader-demand signals across 49 tracked lanes (Reddit, Goodreads, BookTok, AO3) · Cite freely with a link

How to read the numbers. Every figure is a share of voice: the percentage of reader-demand signals in a lane and period that mention a trope. Shares are computed within-period, so the 8× growth of the underlying corpus (12,065 signals in 2023 → 95,923 in 2026 so far) doesn't inflate trends. Coverage of lanes expanded over the three years; the safest comparisons — and the ones we make — are between tropes measured in the same periods.

The pattern: situation tropes (found family, forced proximity, second chance) outlast dynamic tropes (enemies-to-lovers, grumpy/sunshine). Readers tire of a flavour of banter; they never tire of people being stuck together and building a home.

Finding 2 — The conversation fragmented

In 2023 H2 the top ten tropes above accounted for roughly 45% of all trope demand. In 2026 H1 the same ten account for about 25%. That missing share didn't vanish — it moved into the long tail: micro-tropes, cross-genre blends, and hyper-specific asks (our engine now tracks 2,100+ micro-tropes). The era when writing "the big trope" was a strategy by itself is ending; the winners now pair a durable situation trope with a specific, rising hook.

Global trends tell you the weather; your lane tells you whether to bring an umbrella.

What to do with this

If you write romance, the playbook the data suggests: anchor your next book in a durable situation trope (found family, forced proximity, second chance), position it with the specific hook rising in your lane, and avoid leading with a hook that's cooling where you publish — even if it's rising somewhere else.
